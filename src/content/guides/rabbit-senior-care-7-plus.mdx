senior rabbit care is a phase that doesn't get talked about as much as new-rabbit care, but it's where the relationship usually deepens. an 8-year-old rabbit who's been with you since they were a baby is a different being than the skittish young one — calmer, more attached, more aware of routine.

it also requires different care.

## when is "senior"

rough guide by breed:

- giant breeds (Flemish Giant, English Lop): senior at 5
- standard breeds (Holland Lop, Dutch, Mini Rex): senior at 7
- small breeds (Netherland Dwarf, smaller mixes): senior at 7-8

individual variation is large. a healthy 8-year-old can be acting like a 5-year-old. an unhealthy 6-year-old can present as senior. it's the changes you observe that signal the transition more than the calendar.

## the changes you'll see

normal age-related changes:

**1. activity level decreases.**

- shorter free-roam sessions
- more lounging
- still active but in shorter bursts
- preference for napping in warm spots

**2. coat changes.**

- slight thinning, especially around eyes
- sometimes whitening (less common in SG breeds than in Western-line rabbits)
- grooming may be less thorough

**3. weight shifts.**

- some seniors gain weight as activity decreases
- some lose weight gradually as muscle mass declines
- weekly weighing becomes more important

**4. appetite changes.**

- preference for softer foods
- sometimes pellet refusal
- water intake variable

**5. sleep patterns.**

- longer naps
- earlier evening winding down
- sometimes sleeping less deeply

**6. behaviour shifts.**

- more attached to owner (or sometimes withdrawn)
- less interested in new toys
- more set in routine
- less playful

## the vet schedule

senior rabbits benefit from more frequent monitoring:

**ages 7-9:**

- vet checkup every 6 months
- annual blood work and urinalysis
- dental check at every visit
- weight check at every visit

**ages 10+:**

- vet checkup every 4-6 months
- blood work and urinalysis at every visit
- ongoing dental surveillance
- any new symptoms warrant urgent review

## diet adjustments

senior rabbits often benefit from:

- continued hay-first diet, possibly with softer hay options (second-cut timothy is softer)
- fresh greens daily (encourages hydration and nutrient diversity)
- modest pellet portion (1-2 tablespoons daily) unless overweight
- timothy-based pellets, not alfalfa
- adequate water (multiple bowls)
- treats only as bonding tool, not regular dietary additions

watch for:

- weight changes (gain or loss)
- reduced hay intake (often signals dental issues)
- selective eating
- changes in droppings

## environmental modifications

senior rabbits do better with:

**accessible setup:**

- low-sided litter pans (easier to step into)
- non-slip flooring (joints stiffer)
- soft sleep areas (joint comfort)
- ramps if multi-level enclosure

**temperature comfort:**

- AC during hot periods (less tolerance for temperature extremes)
- warm spots for cooler evenings (some seniors prefer warmer)
- quiet, draft-free location

**reduced stress:**

- consistent routine
- familiar surroundings
- minimal disruption from visitors

## the chronic conditions

common senior issues:

**arthritis.**

- stiffness, especially after rest
- reluctance to jump
- weight shifts to favor certain limbs
- vet visit for assessment; sometimes medication helps

**dental issues.**

- accumulating from years of normal wear
- often presents as selective eating
- vet check more frequently

**kidney function decline.**

- detected via blood work
- managed with diet and hydration
- monitoring becomes important

**heart disease.**

- less common but can occur
- vet-detected via auscultation
- managed with medication if present

**reduced immunity.**

- more susceptible to respiratory infections, dental abscesses
- prompt vet response important

## the end-of-life conversation

this is hard to have but important.

at some point, senior rabbits face decisions about quality of life. signs that quality of life is significantly compromised:

- chronic pain not responding to medication
- inability to eat or drink despite vet intervention
- loss of mobility to the point of not moving
- significant chronic illness without improvement
- frequent crisis episodes despite treatment

quality of life is often discussed with the vet over weeks or months, not a single moment. the goal is to make decisions before the rabbit is suffering.

our [end-of-life guide](/care/rabbit-end-of-life-quality-scale/) covers the assessment scale and decision framework.

## the bonded pair consideration

when a senior rabbit dies, the surviving partner often grieves. signs:

- decreased eating for days
- searching the home
- vocalisations
- general withdrawal

most surviving rabbits adjust within weeks. for some, the loss is severe enough that a new bonded pair becomes important. our [partner-loss guide](/care/rabbit-loss-of-partner-grief/) covers this.

## the cost reality

senior rabbit care is more expensive:

- vet visits 2x per year vs annual: doubles vet base cost
- blood work and urinalysis adds $150-300 annually
- medication if chronic conditions develop: $50-200 monthly
- emergency visits more frequent as fragile health develops

budget for senior years: SGD 500-1500 annually in routine costs, more for chronic conditions.

## what owners often get wrong

three patterns from SG senior rabbit owners:

- **maintaining the same diet and routine.** senior rabbits often need adjustment. the diet at 5 may not be appropriate at 10
- **missing subtle changes.** stiffness, slight weight changes, slight appetite changes — these matter more in senior rabbits than younger
- **not preparing for end-of-life decisions.** the conversation often gets delayed. it's healthier to think about it in advance with your vet's input
