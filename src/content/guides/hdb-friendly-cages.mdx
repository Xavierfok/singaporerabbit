a rabbit can absolutely live well in a HDB flat. what doesn't work is the standard pet-shop rabbit cage. this guide covers what actually fits, what to look for, and the patterns SG owners settle into after the first 6 to 12 months.

## the minimum dimensions

baseline cage floor area, by adult rabbit weight:

- **under 1.5 kg** (Netherland Dwarf, juvenile Holland Lop): 0.9 by 0.6 metres
- **1.5 to 2.5 kg** (adult Holland Lop, Mini Rex, Lionhead): 1.2 by 0.6 metres
- **over 2.5 kg** (Mini Lop, Dutch, larger breeds): 1.5 by 0.8 metres

these are minimums for a rabbit that gets 3 to 4 hours of free run-out time daily. larger is better. smaller without compensating run-out time is welfare-compromised.

vertical space matters less than floor area. a rabbit needs to stretch out, hop a step or two, and stand up briefly without hitting the ceiling of the enclosure. a cage 60 cm tall is plenty for most pet breeds.

## why pet-shop "rabbit cages" don't fit

walk into any pet supermarket and the cages labelled "rabbit cage" are typically 90 by 50 cm or smaller. a Holland Lop needs 1.2 by 0.6 metres. the math doesn't work.

three reasons the pet-shop cage is undersized:

- they are designed for guinea pigs, hamsters, or juvenile rabbits
- the price point targets impulse buyers, not informed owners
- shelf space at the retailer rewards smaller cages

if you already bought one, it is fine as a sleeping nook inside a larger enclosure. but it should not be the rabbit's only space.

## the practical SG setup

three layout patterns that work in HDB flats:

**pattern 1: x-pen on tile or vinyl flooring**

- 1.2 by 1.2 metre puppy x-pen (about SGD 60 to 100 from pet supermarkets or online)
- placed on existing tile or vinyl flooring in a corner of the living room or bedroom
- furnished with: hide-out box, hay rack, water bowl or bottle, litter box in one corner
- run-out time: open the x-pen daily for 3 to 4 hours; the rabbit explores the rabbit-proofed area

**pattern 2: free-range in a single room**

- entire room (typically a bedroom or storage room) rabbit-proofed
- no cage; rabbit lives free-range with hide-outs, litter, food, and water at fixed spots
- requires more rabbit-proofing (cables, baseboards, anything chewable)
- works well for owners home most of the day

**pattern 3: hybrid (cage as a base, free-range time)**

- a larger cage (commercial 1.2 metre dog crate or a custom NIC-grid enclosure) as the base
- rabbit spends nights and unsupervised hours in the cage
- free-range hours (3 to 4+) when owner is home
- common compromise for owners who travel or have limited rabbit-proofing capacity

## flooring choices

what works:

- **tile**: existing HDB tile floors are excellent. easy to clean, doesn't trap moisture, doesn't react to rabbit urine (which is alkaline)
- **vinyl/linoleum**: same as tile. some owners install a vinyl mat over carpet to create a rabbit-friendly zone
- **bamboo or hardwood (with caveats)**: works if sealed; rabbit urine can damage unsealed wood
- **kitchen-style mats**: cheap, washable, replaceable

what to avoid:

- **wire-mesh flooring**: causes pressure sores in a few weeks. never appropriate for any rabbit
- **carpet (long-pile)**: traps fur, hides droppings, develops a rabbit-flat-smell that is hard to remove
- **artificial grass**: chewable, gets contaminated fast

if your HDB flat is fully carpeted, place a 2 by 2 metre vinyl mat or tile-effect rug as the rabbit zone.

## rabbit-proofing essentials

a rabbit-safe room or zone needs:

- **cable protection**: any wire within rabbit reach. flexible plastic conduit (split-tube cable cover from hardware shops, ~SGD 1 per metre) prevents chewing
- **baseboard protection**: 1 metre tall plastic or wood barriers along chewable wall sections, particularly skirting boards
- **plant removal**: pothos, philodendron, lily, and many common houseplants are toxic. relocate or remove
- **chemical lockout**: cleaning products, mothballs, anything reachable

an unprepared rabbit room creates expensive problems. a 30-minute rabbit-proofing pass before moving in saves hundreds in vet bills and chewed cables.

## ventilation and AC placement

the rabbit's enclosure should be in the coolest part of the flat with reasonable air circulation.

best spots:

- bedrooms with AC (cooled during peak hours)
- living rooms with ceiling fans plus AC
- corridors with cross-ventilation (only if not direct sunlight)

avoid:

- west-facing rooms (afternoon sun heat retention)
- kitchens (cooking heat plus humidity, plus food access risks)
- balconies and outdoor spaces (illegal for indoor pet rabbits to be left outside; also unsafe in our climate)
- any space with direct AC blast on the rabbit (cold air flowing directly onto the rabbit causes respiratory issues)

for the climate-management plan, see [heat stroke prevention](/care/heat-stroke-prevention/).

## cleaning routine

a HDB-flat-realistic cleaning schedule:

- **daily**: spot-clean litter box, refill water, replenish hay, remove visible droppings outside the litter
- **weekly**: full litter box change, wipe down enclosure surfaces with vinegar-water solution (rabbit-safe), wash water bowl thoroughly
- **monthly**: deep clean of the whole enclosure, scrub away any urine staining on flooring, check for any rabbit-proofing breaches
- **quarterly**: replace any worn-out enclosure pieces (chewed corners, damaged litter boxes)

vinegar diluted 1:5 with water is rabbit-safe and removes urine smell. avoid bleach in the enclosure space; the residue can irritate.

## storage considerations

rabbit supplies take up more space than first-time owners expect.

- a 5 to 10 kg sealed bag of hay
- 1 to 2 kg of pellets
- spare litter, replacement hay racks, grooming tools
- a fan, frozen water bottles, ceramic tiles for cooling

a HDB flat needs a designated rabbit-supply area. a cupboard, the top of a wardrobe, or a corner of the kitchen with shelving works.

## what owners often get wrong

three patterns from SG HDB owner forums:

- **starting with a tiny cage and "we'll upgrade later"**: the rabbit develops cage-related stress and behavioural issues that take months to reverse
- **placing the enclosure in the kitchen or by the fridge**: heat from cooking, humidity, and accessible food create constant management problems
- **assuming the rabbit will be fine free-range without rabbit-proofing**: chewed cables and damaged baseboards are inevitable
