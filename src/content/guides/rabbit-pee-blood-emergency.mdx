spotting red or pink urine in your rabbit's litter tray is one of the more alarming things you can see as an owner. in Singapore, it carries extra weight. exotic vets are scattered across the island, and many general cat-and-dog clinics are not equipped to treat rabbits properly. if it happens at night or on a public holiday, your options narrow quickly. Singapore's ambient temperature stays between 28 and 32°C year-round, with humidity between 70 and 90%. that places your rabbit's kidneys and urinary tract under constant background stress. knowing what to do before you even call a vet can make a real difference.

## what blood in rabbit urine actually looks like

rabbit urine is naturally pigmented. diet, hydration, and plant compounds affect the color; it can range from pale yellow to orange, rust red, or brownish. this confuses a lot of owners, especially newer ones.

true blood in urine is different. look for these signs:

- bright red or dark maroon urine, often with a slightly thicker consistency than usual
- red staining that does not fade or dilute when you splash water on it
- clots or stringy material mixed into the urine
- your rabbit straining or sitting in an unusual position over the tray

if you are unsure, place a white paper towel under the soiled area before cleaning the tray. red pigment from vegetables typically spreads outward evenly, like a watercolour wash. blood tends to stay concentrated and darker in the center of the stain.

if you cannot tell which it is, do not wait. treat it as blood and contact a vet.

## common causes in Singapore rabbits

hematuria, the medical term for blood in urine, has several possible causes. a SG exotic vet will need to determine which one through examination and testing.

**urinary tract infection.** bacteria can travel up the urethra and infect the bladder. female rabbits are more susceptible because of their shorter urethral anatomy. UTIs can escalate quickly in warm conditions, where bacteria multiply faster than in cooler climates.

**urinary sludge or bladder stones.** rabbits that are not drinking enough water develop calcium sludge that irritates or damages the bladder lining. Singapore's heat accelerates fluid loss through rapid breathing. a rabbit that appears comfortable indoors may still be mildly dehydrated if AC is not running consistently.

**uterine bleeding.** unspayed female rabbits are at high risk of uterine disease, including uterine adenocarcinoma. this cancer is extremely common in intact females over two years old. blood may appear in the urine even though the source is the uterus, not the bladder. that alone is a strong reason to spay your rabbit early.

**kidney disease.** the kidneys may bleed internally. this is harder to detect from symptoms alone and requires blood work and imaging to confirm.

**trauma.** a fall from a high surface, even a sofa in an HDB flat, can cause internal bruising. rabbits that free-roam and jump off furniture are more exposed to this risk than many owners realise.

## how Singapore's climate makes this worse

your rabbit does not sweat. it regulates body temperature primarily through blood flow in its ears and through rapid breathing. when ambient temperature stays above 28°C for extended periods, its kidneys work harder to maintain fluid balance throughout the body.

a rabbit in a poorly ventilated HDB flat without AC can pass more concentrated urine. concentrated urine contains more calcium per volume, which raises sludge risk. it also creates a more hospitable environment for bacterial growth.

running AC between 24 and 26°C helps considerably. pair that with at least two water sources: one sipper bottle and one ceramic bowl. Singapore tap water is safe for rabbits and does not need to be replaced with distilled or filtered water. but the water must be fresh; bowls need to be rinsed and refilled daily. a water bowl left out in Singapore's humidity can develop biofilm within 24 hours.

if your rabbit has spent significant time in a non-AC room recently, mild dehydration may be a contributing factor in what you are seeing.

## what to do in the first hour

the first hour matters. here is the sequence to follow.

**step 1.** do not panic, but do not delay either. before cleaning the tray, take a clear photo or short video. vets find this useful when triaging over the phone.

**step 2.** check your rabbit for secondary signs. is it hunched over, grinding its teeth, pressing its belly to the floor, or refusing to move? these suggest pain and increase the urgency level significantly.

**step 3.** check when it last urinated normally. if it has not passed urine in several hours and is straining, that can indicate a blockage. a blockage is an emergency requiring same-hour attention.

**step 4.** call an exotic vet or exotic-capable clinic in Singapore. explain what you observed. describe the color, consistency, and any behavioral changes. ask if they can see your rabbit the same day.

**step 5.** if the earliest appointment is tomorrow, ask the vet what signs would push you to seek an after-hours option tonight. have that answer written down.

do not give your rabbit any human medication. water access stays open. let it drink freely while you arrange the visit.

## what to expect at a SG exotic vet

a SG exotic vet will typically start with a physical examination and a urinalysis. they will likely recommend X-rays or ultrasound to check for bladder sludge, stones, or uterine abnormalities.

as of 2026, a consultation at a Singapore exotic vet typically costs between SGD 60 and SGD 130 for the visit alone. X-rays may add SGD 80 to SGD 200 depending on the clinic and number of views required. ultrasound generally costs more, and blood work to assess kidney function adds further if the vet suspects renal involvement.

bring your rabbit in a well-ventilated carrier. line the bottom with a white towel so any urine produced during transit is visible. keep the carrier out of direct sun. if you are taking public transport, avoid travelling during peak hours when crowding and jostling add stress. MRT air-conditioning is generally sufficient for short journeys.

## what owners often get wrong

**waiting to see if it clears up on its own.** blood in urine does not reliably self-resolve in rabbits. even if the next litter tray looks normal, the underlying cause is still present. one clear tray is not confirmation that the problem has passed.

**assuming it must be diet pigment.** red pigment from leafy vegetables is real. if you have not introduced new foods recently and the urine looks different from usual, diet is the less likely explanation. the safer assumption is blood until a vet says otherwise.

**not having a vet contact ready before an emergency.** many SG owners only search for exotic vets when something goes wrong. finding one who is available and experienced with rabbits under time pressure is harder than it sounds. build a short contact list now, including at least one after-hours option, before you need it.

**ignoring that the rabbit is unspayed.** if your rabbit is female and unspayed, uterine disease is among the most likely causes of blood near or in the urine. the risk increases sharply after age two. Singapore exotic vets generally recommend spaying between four and six months of age, before the first heat cycle.
