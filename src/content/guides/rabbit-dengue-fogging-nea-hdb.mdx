the SMS arrives on a Tuesday afternoon, short and official-looking: your block is in an active NEA dengue cluster and thermal fogging will be conducted the following morning between 7am and 9am. most residents scan it and go back to whatever they were doing. if you own a rabbit, you stop. you read it again. you start mentally running through your flat — the louvre windows you never fully close, the balcony door with the gap at the bottom, the corner of the living room where your bun spends every morning. that low-grade panic is the right instinct. act on it.

rabbit owners in Singapore have largely not been given a clear playbook for dengue fogging. the NEA advisories are written for human residents. the pest control leaflets focus on mosquito eradication. nobody writes the guide that explains what pyrethroid insecticides do to a 1.5kg body with lungs the size of your thumb and a grooming habit that transfers whatever lands on fur directly to the gut. this article is that guide. it covers the science, the preparation window, the day-of protocol, the cleanup, and the vet response when something goes wrong.

## what NEA fogging actually is

NEA uses three methods to suppress adult mosquito populations in active dengue cluster zones. understanding which one is happening near your flat matters because exposure risk differs significantly.

thermal fogging is the most common method in HDB estates. a petrol-powered machine heats a diluted insecticide solution into a visible white fog that drifts through void decks, corridors, staircases, and open windows. the fog hangs in the air for 15-30 minutes before dispersing. it is visually dramatic and is the one most likely to penetrate indoor spaces through gaps and ventilation paths if windows are not sealed.

ultra-low volume (ULV) cold fogging uses compressed air to create a fine mist without heat. the droplet size is smaller than thermal fog, which means the particles stay airborne longer and travel further. NEA uses ULV foggers in enclosed areas like carparks and void decks. penetration into closed flats is lower than thermal fogging, but not zero.

ground spray is direct application of insecticide to drains, grass areas, and surfaces where mosquitoes rest. this is the least likely to affect indoor animals but becomes relevant if your rabbit has balcony access and the spray team works the corridor or common area directly outside.

for most HDB residents, the primary risk is thermal fogging. the protocol in this guide is calibrated to that scenario. if you are in a dengue cluster zone and receive a notice, assume thermal fogging unless stated otherwise and prepare accordingly.

## what's in the fog — pyrethroid insecticides

NEA's registered insecticides for dengue fogging are predominantly pyrethroid compounds. the most commonly used in Singapore include deltamethrin, permethrin, and prallethrin. you may also see cypermethrin and lambda-cyhalothrin listed on pest control documentation.

pyrethroids are synthetic derivatives of pyrethrins, the natural insecticide extracted from chrysanthemum flowers. they work by disrupting the sodium channels in insect nerve cells, causing continuous nerve firing, paralysis, and death. they are extraordinarily effective at mosquito-scale doses and degrade relatively quickly in sunlight and air — which is why NEA uses them in outdoor and semi-enclosed spaces.

the problem is that "relatively safe for humans and birds" does not extend to small mammals. reptiles and aquatic animals are also highly sensitive, but rabbits sit in a particularly vulnerable category alongside cats. the liver enzyme pathways that break down pyrethroids in humans and dogs are either absent or significantly reduced in lagomorphs. a dose that causes mild skin tingling in a human resident walking through the corridor can cause neurological symptoms in a rabbit exposed to the same concentration indoors.

deltamethrin, NEA's most frequently deployed compound, binds to voltage-gated sodium channels in nerve membranes. at sub-lethal doses it causes the characteristic CS syndrome (choreoathetosis with salivation) — tremors, drooling, and hypersalivation. at higher doses it progresses to seizures. permethrin, the compound also found in some louse treatments and fabric treatments, is acutely toxic to cats and rabbits at concentrations that are considered safe for dogs and humans.

the fog concentration during NEA fogging is calibrated to be sub-lethal for larger mammals at normal outdoor exposure. the risk for rabbits comes from indoor accumulation, prolonged exposure time, respiratory concentration (a rabbit breathes faster than a human relative to body weight), and post-exposure grooming.

## why rabbits are particularly vulnerable

four biological factors make rabbits significantly more sensitive to pyrethroid exposure than the human residents the NEA advisories are written for.

first, body size and surface-area-to-volume ratio. a 1.5kg rabbit has proportionally more skin relative to its body mass than a 70kg adult. airborne droplets that land on fur represent a much larger relative dose per kilogram of body weight. a thin insecticide mist that deposits negligible residue on a person's arm deposits a meaningful dose on a rabbit's entire coat.

second, respiratory rate and tidal volume. rabbits breathe between 30 and 60 times per minute at rest, compared to 12-20 breaths per minute in a resting adult human. they are obligate nasal breathers. their olfactory system is highly sensitive. when airborne pyrethroid particles enter a space, a rabbit in that room is inhaling the contaminated air at twice to four times the rate of a human, drawing particles deeper into the respiratory tract.

third, the absence of a vomit reflex. rabbits physically cannot vomit. in humans and dogs, accidental ingestion of a toxin often triggers emesis, reducing total absorbed dose. in rabbits, anything that reaches the gut stays there and continues to be absorbed. this makes the grooming pathway — where a rabbit methodically licks pyrethroid residue from its coat — a direct ingestion route with no safety mechanism.

fourth, hepatic metabolism differences. the cytochrome P450 enzymes responsible for breaking down pyrethroids are less active in rabbits than in most mammals studied. degradation is slower, which means the compound stays active in the body longer at a given dose.

combine these four factors and you have an animal that absorbs more, inhales more, cannot purge what it ingests, and processes the toxin slowly. this is why the standard advice of "keep pets indoors during fogging" is necessary but not sufficient for rabbit owners.

## how to know when fogging is scheduled

Singapore's dengue cluster management moves quickly. a cluster can be declared and fogging scheduled within 48-72 hours of NEA identifying a case cluster in your block or surrounding blocks. knowing the advance notice channels gives you the maximum possible preparation window.

the NEA dengue cluster SMS is the fastest official channel. when your residential address is registered in an active cluster zone, NEA sends an SMS notification to the mobile number associated with your residential address (typically through your SingPass-linked contact). this SMS typically arrives 24-48 hours before the scheduled operation but can arrive the same morning. keep your SingPass contact details current.

letterbox notices are the fallback channel when SMS delivery fails or the fogging is organized at grassroots level through the Town Council. these are physical A5 notices, often left by pest control operators or NEA officers, with the date, time, and method of treatment. check your letterbox the evening before if there is any dengue activity in your estate.

the MyENV app (available on iOS and Android) has a dengue cluster map that shows active clusters updated daily. you can see whether your block or neighboring blocks have been flagged. enable push notifications in the app so cluster declarations near your address trigger an alert. this gives you proactive visibility rather than waiting for the SMS.

the NEA website at nea.gov.sg has an interactive dengue cluster map that mirrors the app data. if you prefer a desktop view or want to share a link, the map shows cluster boundaries by postal sector.

social media and neighborhood channels — Telegram groups for your HDB block, Next Door, Facebook community groups — are often faster than official channels for sharing notices posted in corridors or void decks. join your estate's community group if you are not already in it.

## the 24-hour prep checklist

when you receive a fogging notice, you have a limited window to prepare. the following checklist is organized by task priority. work through it in order if you have a full 24 hours. if you receive shorter notice, prioritize windows and ventilation above everything else.

**verify the scope.** check which blocks and which floors are being treated. a notice covering blocks 100-120 in your estate does not necessarily mean your block. confirm your block number. if your block is not directly listed but is adjacent to the treatment zone, still prepare — thermal fog drifts.

**identify all air entry points in your flat.** walk through every room. note which windows are louvre type (multiple angled glass slats with gaps between them), casement type (hinged panel, seals better when closed), or sliding type (two-panel, gap at track when closed). louvre windows are the highest risk because the gaps between slats cannot be fully sealed by simply closing the handle.

**purchase supplies if not already stocked.** at minimum you need: drafting tape or painter's tape, garbage bags, a damp cloth, and ideally a HEPA air purifier. most NTUC FairPrice and Giant stores carry tape and bags. air purifiers are available at Courts, Harvey Norman, Lazada same-day, and Shopee express.

**charge your HEPA purifier** and confirm it has a clean filter. a dirty filter reduces effectiveness significantly.

**prepare your rabbit's temporary indoor space.** identify the room in your flat furthest from the street-facing and corridor-facing windows. this is usually an inner bedroom or the bathroom side of the flat. set up your rabbit's pen, water, and hay in that room the night before.

**brief anyone else in your household.** they need to know not to open windows during the fogging period and not to let the rabbit out into common areas before the post-fogging cleanup is complete.

## sealing windows and openings

sealing is the most labor-intensive part of the preparation and the one most rabbit owners underestimate. the goal is not to achieve a hermetic seal — that is neither necessary nor possible in a standard HDB flat — but to significantly reduce the volume of airborne insecticide that enters your space during the 30-60 minute fogging window.

**louvre windows** are the hardest to seal. each glass slat has a small gap at the top and bottom when closed. use long strips of painter's tape or drafting tape applied horizontally across the outside face of the slats where they overlap the frame. you do not need to tape every individual slat — focus on the frame perimeter and the top and bottom of the louvre panel. if you have an old bedsheet or large garbage bag, tape it over the outside of the louvre frame as an additional barrier.

**casement windows** seal better when the latch is fully engaged. still tape the perimeter gap between the window frame and the wall reveal, and tape over any ventilation slots at the top of the frame if present.

**sliding windows** have a track gap at the bottom center where the two panels meet. run a strip of tape along this center seam. tape the perimeter where the frame meets the wall.

**kitchen exhaust fans** and rangehoods with external venting should have the shutter or flap taped closed from inside. if your kitchen exhaust duct exits through the wall at corridor level, this is a significant entry point during fogging.

**balcony sliding doors** — tape the center seam and the gap at the bottom track. if there are louvre panels on the balcony parapet wall, seal these the same way as interior louvre windows.

**gap under the front door** — the gap between the bottom of your front door and the HDB corridor floor is a low path that fog drifts through. roll a damp towel and lay it along the base of the door. if you have a foam draft excluder, use that instead.

**toilets with windows** — bathroom windows are frequently overlooked. seal them the same way as bedroom windows. the toilet exhaust fan that vents to the external duct should also be taped over if it has a shutter.

after sealing, do a visual check of each room. run your hand along sealed edges on a calm day — if you feel airflow, the seal is incomplete. add another tape layer.

## the AC strategy

air conditioning in HDB flats introduces complexity because the wall-mounted split units common in Singapore draw air from outside through the compressor unit on the exterior ledge. during fogging, an AC unit running in cooling mode cycles refrigerant but does not typically introduce outdoor air into the indoor coil space — the indoor unit recirculates room air over the evaporator coil and the compressor sits outside. this means standard split-unit AC in recirculate mode is generally safe to run during fogging.

however, there are important caveats. some split units have a "fresh air" or "ventilation" mode that partially opens a damper to draw outdoor air. disable this mode during fogging. check your remote's mode settings — if you see a "fresh air" button or a ventilation symbol, press it to off before the fogging starts.

portable air conditioners and window units are a different story. window-unit ACs draw outdoor air directly into the indoor space. turn these off and seal the unit's air intake with tape and a garbage bag for the duration of fogging.

if you do not run AC during fogging, your flat will be warmer, especially in the morning before Singapore's heat peaks. this is fine for rabbits for 2-3 hours but make sure they have fresh water and are not in direct sunlight through any unsealed window. rabbit heat tolerance is limited — do not sacrifice ventilation control to keep the flat cool. seal the flat, use AC if it is a recirculate-only split unit, and prioritize your rabbit's safety over comfort.

## the HEPA filter strategy

a HEPA filter is your secondary line of defense inside the sealed flat. it captures airborne particles down to 0.3 microns at 99.97% efficiency. pyrethroid droplets in thermal fog range from 1-10 microns. a running HEPA purifier in the room where your rabbit is housed will progressively filter any particles that have entered, reducing the airborne concentration over time.

popular models in Singapore available at FairPrice Xtra, Harvey Norman, and online:

- **Xiaomi Mi Air Purifier 4 / 4 Pro** — price range SGD 120-200, widely stocked on Lazada and Shopee, replacement filters available, compact form factor suitable for HDB bedrooms.
- **Philips AC series** (AC1715, AC2887) — SGD 150-350, available at Courts and Harvey Norman, well-documented HEPA filtration specs.
- **Sharp FU series** — SGD 180-400, includes plasmacluster ionizer, available at Best Denki.
- **Honeywell HPA series** — SGD 200-350, available on Lazada.
- **Mistral** — budget option SGD 80-120 at FairPrice, HEPA layer included, suitable for smaller rooms.

position the air purifier as close to your rabbit's pen as practical, with the intake facing the room rather than pointing directly at the rabbit. set it to maximum speed during the fogging window and for 2-3 hours after. if you have two units, place one near the highest-risk window and one near your rabbit. replace or check the filter after a fogging event — the filter may have captured insecticide residue and should be discarded if the fogging was heavy.

if you do not own an air purifier and cannot get one before the fogging, place a portable fan blowing across a damp towel hung in the doorway of the room. this is a rough mechanical filter and far less effective than HEPA, but it provides some particle capture in a pinch.

## the day-of protocol

on the morning of the fogging, execute the following in order before 7am or at least 30 minutes before the scheduled start time.

move your rabbit to the pre-prepared inner room. do not let them roam the living room or near windows during setup. close and latch the inner room door.

complete the window and door sealing if you have not done so the night before. run tape over any gaps you identified the previous day.

turn on the HEPA purifier in your rabbit's room. set it to maximum speed.

check your rabbit's water bottle and top it up. stress and mild respiratory exposure both increase fluid loss. ensure they have adequate hay for the fogging window.

switch your split-unit AC to cooling mode and verify no "fresh air" mode is active. turn off any exhaust fans or bathroom ventilators.

stay indoors during the fogging window. do not open any door or window while fogging is active. you can monitor the fogging from inside — the visible white fog in the corridor or void deck is your cue that the window is active. wait until the fog has fully cleared and at least 30 minutes have passed before any ventilation.

monitor your rabbit visually through the inner room window or by quietly entering and checking every 30 minutes. you are looking for normal posture, normal breathing, and normal activity for the time of day. do not handle the rabbit excessively during this period as handling adds stress.

## the post-fogging cleanup

the fogging window is the acute exposure phase, but pyrethroid residue on surfaces remains active and accessible to rabbits through grooming and paw contact for hours after the fog clears. the post-fogging cleanup phase is non-negotiable before you allow your rabbit back into common areas.

**timing.** wait a minimum of 4-8 hours after the fogging ends before reopening windows. NEA's public guidance for human residents is typically 30 minutes to 1 hour for ventilation, but that guidance is not written for animals that groom themselves. if the fogging ended at 9am, do not open windows and release your rabbit before 1-5pm.

**ventilate before you clean.** open all windows and doors (except the inner room where your rabbit is housed) and allow cross-ventilation for at least 45-60 minutes before you start surface cleaning. this disperses any residual airborne particles. do not release your rabbit into the ventilated flat until surface cleaning is complete.

**wipe all horizontal surfaces.** any surface at or below rabbit-accessible height needs to be wiped with a damp microfibre cloth. this includes floors, skirting boards, low furniture surfaces, playpen structures, and baseboards. change the cloth frequently — you are removing residue, not spreading it. use plain water; do not use citrus-based cleaners or essential oil cleaners, as many of these are independently toxic to rabbits.

**mop hard floors.** use a clean mop with plain water or a mild pet-safe floor cleaner. allow floors to dry completely before rabbit access. wet floors mean your rabbit will walk through the cleaning solution and groom it from their feet.

**vacuum soft surfaces.** sofas, rugs, carpet areas, and fabric pen inserts should be vacuumed. empty the vacuum receptacle outside the flat immediately.

**check the rabbit's pen and accessories.** water bottle nozzle, hay rack, ceramic food bowls, and any plastic accessories should be wiped and rinsed. if the pen was in a room that was not fully sealed, wash all fabric accessories in the washing machine.

**do a final air quality check before rabbit access.** if you own a VOC sensor or air quality monitor, check that readings have returned to baseline before releasing your rabbit from the inner room. if you do not have one, use your own nose — if you can smell anything chemical in the room, continue ventilating.

only after completing all of the above should you open the inner room door and allow your rabbit to roam.

## the signs of pyrethroid toxicity in rabbits

even with careful preparation, some degree of exposure may occur if your flat is old, your windows are poorly fitting, or the fogging crew was operating on your level rather than the void deck. knowing the early and late signs of pyrethroid toxicity means you call the vet at the right moment rather than waiting until your rabbit is in crisis.

early signs (appearing within 1-3 hours of exposure):

- **hypersalivation and drooling.** excessive wetness around the chin and dewlap, visible saliva strings. this is one of the first and most consistent signs of pyrethroid exposure in small mammals.
- **twitching or fine muscle tremors.** particularly around the face, ears, and limbs. you may notice rapid ear movements or whisker quivering that is not normal grooming behavior.
- **hyperactivity or agitation.** the rabbit may pace, thump repeatedly, or appear unable to settle. this precedes the later depressive phase.
- **dilated pupils.** in normal indoor lighting, rabbit pupils should be moderately sized. significantly dilated pupils in a room that is not dark suggest neurological involvement.
- **excessive grooming.** if your rabbit begins grooming more intensively than usual immediately after you open the inner room, this may indicate skin irritation from residue on their coat.

late signs (appearing 3-8 hours after significant exposure, indicating serious toxicity):

- **ataxia and weakness.** the rabbit stumbles, cannot maintain balance, or sits in a hunched position with limbs splayed. this indicates neurological involvement.
- **seizures.** full-body convulsions. this is a veterinary emergency. do not wait.
- **labored breathing.** open-mouth breathing in a rabbit is always an emergency sign. combined with possible pyrethroid exposure, this requires immediate vet attention.
- **loss of consciousness or unresponsiveness.** the rabbit does not respond to sound or touch.

if you observe any late sign, do not wait to see if it resolves. call your vet immediately. if it is outside regular hours, call the nearest 24-hour emergency vet in Singapore.

## the vet response if exposure is suspected

if you suspect pyrethroid exposure — either because you observed signs or because you know your flat was inadequately sealed during fogging — contact your vet and tell them specifically: pyrethroid insecticide, thermal fogging, rabbit. this information changes the triage priority and the treatment protocol.

**decontamination bath.** if the rabbit's coat may carry residue, the vet will typically administer a gentle bath with mild dish soap (Dawn or equivalent) to remove surface insecticide before it is further absorbed. if you cannot reach a vet within 30-60 minutes and you have confirmed residue on the coat (visible wet spots, chemical smell), you can initiate a gentle, warm-water rinse of the coat at home while arranging transport — but do not bathe a rabbit that is already showing neurological signs, as the stress may worsen their condition. see [rabbit bathing guidance](/care/rabbit-bathing-when-needed-sg/) for safe technique.

**activated charcoal.** if ingestion via grooming is suspected and the rabbit is still conscious and not seizing, vets may administer activated charcoal orally to bind unabsorbed toxin in the gut. this is administered at the clinic, not at home.

**supportive IV fluids.** pyrethroid toxicity causes fluid and electrolyte disruption. IV fluid support helps maintain kidney function and supports detoxification. this is typically administered over several hours in a hospital setting.

**anticonvulsant medication.** if seizures are occurring, diazepam or midazolam may be administered to control convulsive activity while the toxin clears. the rabbit will be monitored closely for respiratory depression.

**temperature regulation.** toxic rabbits often become hypothermic. the vet will monitor and manage body temperature with heat support.

there is no specific antidote for pyrethroid poisoning. treatment is entirely supportive and aimed at keeping the rabbit stable while their body metabolizes and excretes the compound. the outcome depends heavily on the dose absorbed and how quickly treatment was initiated.

## the long-term recovery

rabbits that receive prompt supportive care and survive the acute toxicity phase generally recover fully. pyrethroids do not cause permanent organ damage at the doses typical of fogging exposure, provided the exposure was limited and treatment was early.

in the days following treatment, monitor appetite and GI motility closely. pyrethroid toxicity and the stress of hospitalization both suppress GI motility, which can trigger GI stasis — a secondary emergency in rabbits. ensure your rabbit is eating hay and producing cecotropes within 12-24 hours of returning home. if gut sounds are absent or the rabbit stops eating, contact your vet immediately. see [rabbit respiratory issues and related care](/care/rabbit-respiratory-issues-singapore/) for recovery monitoring context.

keep the rabbit's environment calm and predictable for 1-2 weeks post-incident. limit handling, avoid introducing new animals, and maintain temperature between 18-26°C. if your rabbit is on any regular medication, confirm with your vet whether dosing schedule changes are needed during recovery given altered liver processing from the toxic event. practical guidance on administering medications is at [rabbit medication administration](/care/rabbit-medication-administration-sg/).

## what owners often get wrong

these are the most common mistakes rabbit owners make during and after dengue fogging in HDB estates.

- **releasing the rabbit one hour after fogging ends.** fog that is no longer visible is not necessarily inert. surface residue persists for hours. the "fog is gone" visual cue does not mean the flat is safe for your rabbit.
- **forgetting the kitchen exhaust.** the kitchen exhaust duct is one of the highest-volume air pathways in an HDB flat and is routinely unsealed during preparation. it connects directly to the corridor-level venting, which is where pest control teams often begin fogging.
- **using citrus or essential oil cleaners for the post-fogging wipe.** these seem like "natural" options but limonene (in citrus cleaners) and many essential oils (tea tree, eucalyptus, peppermint) are independently toxic to rabbits. use plain water and a microfibre cloth.
- **bathing a rabbit that is already showing neurological signs.** the instinct to wash off residue is correct, but a rabbit that is twitching, ataxic, or seizing is too fragile for bath stress. transport immediately, let the vet manage decontamination.
- **not informing the vet that the cause is specifically pyrethroid.** "my rabbit looks unwell" delays triage. "thermal fogging, pyrethroid insecticide, estimated 2 hours ago" puts the vet on the right protocol immediately.
- **assuming the inner room is fully safe without a HEPA filter.** closing the door to the inner room reduces exposure but does not eliminate it. air moves under doors, through AC ducts, and through electrical outlet gaps. a running HEPA purifier in that room materially reduces residual concentration.
